The audit made it readable. This makes it obvious.

The first assessment fixed how the platform looks — surfaces, contrast, type, one visual language. This round fixes how it works: what to do next, how to learn it, and what matters most on every screen. Six prioritised improvements, each shown before and after, in the chosen Kit (Light) system.

00 — The thesis

A pretty screen still leaves people asking “…so what do I do now?”

The Platform Assessment solved the visual problems: the near-black surfaces, the three competing visual languages, the type that was too small. Those fixes land in the Kit system. But a clean surface can still be confusing. Across the pilot, the friction that remains is behavioural, not cosmetic — managers can’t tell what’s urgent, new users are dropped into a fully-loaded dashboard with no guidance, and every number on a row shouts at the same volume.

These six improvements sit on top of the original six. They don’t touch the palette or the type pairing — they change what the interface asks of a person at each moment. Three principles drive all of them.

PRINCIPLE 01

Every screen answers “what now?”

One unmissable next action per view, anchored to the deadline. Everything else is navigation, not a decision.

PRINCIPLE 02

Teach in context, never up front

No tutorial wall. A first-run checklist and live empty states coach people through the one task in front of them.

PRINCIPLE 03

One number leads, the rest support

Typographic roles give every row a single entry point for the eye. Mono is for labels, not for everything.

01
Critical Primary actions

There is no single “next action”

Inside a league the manager is met with four entry points of identical weight — Manage Squad, Market, Bets, Feed — and a deadline that ticks by as a faint line of text in the corner. Nothing on the screen says this is the thing that’s urgent right now. New managers freeze; returning managers miss the deadline and field a broken team. The most consequential moment in fantasy — locking your squad before kick-off — has no home on the screen.

Recommendation

Promote the deadline to a single action banner.

Collapse the competing buttons into tabs and give the screen one unmistakable primary action, tied to the live deadline.

  • Countdown + the exact task: “Set your GW13 squad.”
  • One filled primary button; everything else demoted to tabs.
  • State-aware: flips to “Squad locked ✓” once done.

02
Critical Onboarding

New managers are dropped into the deep end

First login lands on the full dashboard — leaderboard, feed, bets, market — but with an empty squad, no rank, and no instructions. The most important early tasks (join a league, pick fifteen players, choose a captain) are scattered behind navigation a new user hasn’t learned yet. There’s no sense of progress and no obvious first step. The learning curve is a cliff, and it’s where pilots quietly lose people.

Recommendation

A first-run checklist that teaches by doing.

Until setup is complete, the dashboard yields to a three-step checklist. Each step is the real task, done in place — not a tutorial overlay.

  • 1 · Join or create a league
  • 2 · Pick your 15 players
  • 3 · Choose your captain

Once complete, it collapses to a slim progress chip. Empty states elsewhere coach the same way.

03
Moderate Hierarchy

Everything shouts at the same volume

On a standings row the rank, the team name, the gameweek score and the season total are all rendered in roughly the same size and weight — and most of it in JetBrains Mono, which the pilot uses for nearly every value. With no typographic hierarchy, the eye has no entry point. Scanning for “where am I and how am I doing” takes real effort on a screen that should answer it instantly.

Recommendation

Give every row one lead, one support, one label.

A three-tier type system the whole product reuses:

  • Lead — the number that matters (Archivo Black, accent).
  • Support — name in Archivo Black, secondary stats quiet.
  • Label — mono, small, uppercase. For labels only.

04
Moderate Hierarchy

Density without focus

The Player Market shows everything at once — price, form, total points, ownership, fixtures, value change — six values crammed onto every row. The data is good, but it’s undifferentiated, so the one number relevant to this task (can I afford them?) is buried among five you don’t need until you’re deciding. The list reads like a spreadsheet, not a place to make a choice.

Recommendation

Lead with the task number. Reveal the rest on tap.

Progressive disclosure: the row carries only what you need to scan; everything else lives in a player drawer one tap away.

  • Row: position chip, name, form, and the price.
  • Tap → drawer with full stats, fixtures, and the action.
  • Same drawer pattern from Market, Squad and Leaderboard.

05
Moderate Confidence

The system stays silent

Make a transfer, set a captain, place a bet — and the interface barely reacts. The list quietly re-renders and you’re left wondering: did that save? Without confirmation or a sense of consequence, managers re-check their squad, repeat actions, or worse, assume something worked when it didn’t. Fantasy is a game of small, reversible decisions; the interface should make each one feel done and show what it changed.

Recommendation

Confirm every action and preview its impact.

Two lightweight feedback patterns, applied consistently:

  • A confirmation toast with a one-tap Undo.
  • A live impact preview before committing a change.
  • Saved-state markers so “locked” always looks locked.

06
Moderate Mobile

Mobile is a shrunk desktop

On a phone the desktop tables come along unchanged — five columns squeezed edge to edge, text dropping to 9–10px, and a horizontal scroll to reach the numbers that matter. The primary action sits at the top of a tall page, out of thumb reach, while most fantasy management actually happens on mobile during the commute. The most-used breakpoint gets the least-considered layout.

Recommendation

Rebuild for the thumb, not the spreadsheet.

Mobile gets its own patterns from the same system — not a scaled table.

  • Standings as cards: rank, name, one lead number.
  • Bottom tab bar for product nav; strip tabs inside league.
  • Primary action as a thumb-reachable button / FAB.

07 — White label

None of this is branded. It just re-skins.

Every pattern in this document is built on the same three swappable values — accent colour, wordmark, data. The UX improvements ride on top of that: the action banner, the checklist, the type roles and the feedback toasts are identical whichever brand owns the screen. 08 — Sequencing

Ship the cheap clarity wins first.
